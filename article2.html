Learning to code

A year ago, I was packing up my things in France, saying goodbye to my students and returning to London after seven months of assisting in English language teaching. Today, I am working from home and learning to code in my spare time. Although the view from my window doesn’t match the greenery of Burgundy forests, I'm just as challenged in my life as I was then. I swapped French grammar that was full of exceptions to many, many rules for languages that strictly follow the rules they’re built on.

Like most millennials, I’ve grown up with technology and, like most young teens, I was the designated tech-related problem solver in my family. This and the fact that when I was 10, I used my mum’s card to buy credit on an online game called Stardoll. My mum has never been able to fully comprehend how her adolescent child could make a payment online while she had trouble navigating the menu on the cash machine. Although my passion for technology is quite recent, I’ve always been interested to know where things come from and how they are made – I’m (only now) not ashamed to admit that I have lost hours watching How It’s Made on YouTube. My inquisitive nature is what led me move to France without knowing any French and it’s what motivates me to learn how to code without so much as a smidgen of experience in STEM.

At school there wasn't much of an emphasis on technology, so I focused on the humanities subjects I enjoyed and ultimately graduated with a degree in English literature. My more active interest in technology began in 2019 when I enrolled to study a master’s in Digital Asset and Media Management at King's College - a course on digital media studies and research methods. In a module called Web Technologies, I wrote my first line of code and it felt good to see 'bananas' printed on the plain web page in Times New Roman. Once I realised that all my involvement in group projects were tech related, I made the decision to withdraw from the course and pursue coding independently.

I didn’t know where to start but I knew that technology was pioneering a change in gender diversity and I was interested to find out more. I first heard about CodeFirst:Girls while I was serving jury duty. I was speaking about my newfound interest in technology and a fellow juror spoke to me about their daughter being involved in an organisation that teaches women how to code. The rest is history! Well, not quite. I applied to the free community courses organised by Cf:G and partner organisations. I got rejected twice before I successfully enrolled to learn to code Python - the Level 2 course recommended to be taken after the Level 1 Introduction to Front-end Development. So, my start to programming was, in a way, backwards.

I enjoyed the learning environment very much - it was a supportive and encouraging place. It felt good to meet many different women with various educational and work backgrounds knowing that my English literature degree was just as relevant as a computer science one. However, as I was one of the only two people to skip the recommended prerequisite course, learning Python was not as easy as typing /h1/bananas /h1/ in Brackets editor. But I sat next to an intelligent woman who supported me a lot and calmly managed my incessant whispered Q&As. She also told me that learning to code is learning to think differently about information. Coming from a creative background, I was used to interpreting meaning as opposed to taking information at face-value. Eventually, it became obvious that a computer is, indeed, just a machine that wants to be told what to do.
